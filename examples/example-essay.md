## What this note is

In every game I play — League of Legends, Overwatch, even when I picked up bass briefly — I gravitate to the role that absorbs pressure rather than delivers it. Tanks. Front-liners. Rhythm-section. This pattern is consistent enough across unrelated domains that it's not a play-style preference. It's a psychological signature that's leaking into my taste. This note is an attempt to articulate what that signature actually is.

## What I pick

### League of Legends (top lane)

- **Ornn** — forge, smithed items, ult initiation. Builds the team's tools.
- **Garen** — heavy presence, simple, undeniable
- **Maokai** — CC bag. Locks opponents during teamfights
- **Shen** — global ult, the guardian who teleports to save
- **Sett** — hits and gets hit; gets stronger by absorbing damage

### Overwatch (tank role)

- **Sigma** — barrier placement and zone control. The thinking tank
- **Doomfist** — entry tank, drills into space rather than holding it
- **Reinhardt** — shield-and-stand, the most archetypal tank in the game

### Music (band)

- **Drums** — I sing as my main, dabbled in guitar and bass, but my eye always drifts to the drum kit. The instrument I most want to learn next is drums.

## Why tanks — a psychological reading

### Stability as a need — "shaken but standing"

Tanks don't die when they take a hit. The heaviness gives a margin. Health bar dropping doesn't trigger panic; there's room to react. I think I want, in the fictional space of a game, to *be* the thing that doesn't get shaken.

This is interesting because outside games I'm fairly anxious. Maybe the game character is compensatory — letting me practice the experience of unshakeability that I don't naturally have.

### Holding space for others — the keystone position

The tank's job is to take what was aimed at the squishier teammate. There's a clear ethical shape to that: I absorb so that you can act. The drummer has the same shape — the kick holds the floor, and everyone else's groove sits on top.

I notice I do the same in non-game social settings. I gravitate to the position of "be the one who holds the room together" rather than "be the one who performs in front of the room." Drums over guitar. Bass over melody. Tank over carry.

### Process over spotlight

Carries finish kills, get the highlight reel. Tanks build the conditions for the kill. Their contribution is **structural, not climactic**. I think I prefer this kind of contribution generally — building the conditions for something else to succeed is more satisfying to me than being the success itself.

This connects to a separate observation: I find writing tools and writing infrastructure more satisfying than writing finished content. Tooling is tanking. Content is carrying.

### What I'm avoiding

Negative framing: tanks are also a way to not have to be brilliant. A carry has to make plays. A tank's job is positional and steady — measurable but not flashy. Picking tank is partly choosing a role where being *consistent and average* is success.

I'm not sure how I feel about this. It might be self-protective. It might be self-knowledge. Both can be true.

## What I'd test against this reading

> If this reading is right, I should also gravitate to:
> - Drummers and bassists as my favorite musicians in bands I love
> - Co-founder / operations roles over founder / public-facing roles
> - Structural / architectural code over feature code
> - Friendships where I'm the steady one rather than the dramatic one

Looking at this list, the first three are clearly true. The fourth... I think yes, but it's worth examining whether I'm *choosing* that role or accidentally being assigned it because I read as steady.

## What this changes

Not much, immediately. But the pattern recognition is useful:

- When I'm choosing what to work on, the "structural/holding" framing predicts what I'll enjoy
- When I'm assessing a relationship dynamic, "am I tanking here?" is a useful question
- When I notice I'm *not* enjoying something, "is this a carry role I got pushed into?" is worth asking

The deeper question — whether the tanking instinct is a strength or a limitation — I'm leaving open. Probably both, contextually.
